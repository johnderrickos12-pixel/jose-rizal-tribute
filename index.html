Dr. Jose Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda

The Quintessential Filipino: A Deep Dive into the Life, Works, and Legacy of the National Hero

Introduction

Jose Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines, lived a life dedicated to fighting for justice and freedom through peaceful reforms and intellectual enlightenment. This comprehensive tribute delves into every significant aspect of his extraordinary journey, from his birth in Calamba to his tragic execution, and explores the profound legacy he left for his nation.

The Boy from Calamba: Roots of a Hero (1861-1872)

  • Born: June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna.
  • Seventh of eleven children to Francisco Engracio Rizal Mercado y Alejandro and Teodora Morales Alonso Realonda y Quintos.
  • A well-to-do family of mixed Chinese, Japanese, and Spanish ancestry, who leased land from the Dominican friars.
  • Early talents: Drawing, sculpting, poetry at a young age.
  • Influences: His mother's intellect, his brother Paciano's wisdom, and the injustices witnessed (e.g., GomBurZa execution in 1872).

Awakening Intellect: Manila's Halls of Learning (1872-1882)

  • Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872-1877):
    • Initially denied entry due to frail physique; advocated by Manuel Xerez Burgos.
    • Excelled in all subjects, graduated with highest honors (Sobresaliente).
    • Wrote "My First Inspiration" (Mi Primera Inspiración) and "To the Filipino Youth" (A La Juventud Filipina).
  • University of Santo Tomas (1878-1882):
    • Studied Philosophy and Letters, then Ophthalmology.
    • Felt discrimination and hostility from Dominican professors.
    • Began to conceive of his future path to fight for his people.

First Sojourn in Europe: Seeking Knowledge and Freedom (1882-1887)

  • Secret departure for Spain in May 1882 (without parents' knowledge, but with Paciano's support).
  • University Central de Madrid:
    • Completed medical studies and Philosophy and Letters (Licenciado en Filosofía y Letras).
    • Also studied painting, sculpture, languages (German, French, English).
  • Paris & Germany:
    • Worked as an ophthalmologist's assistant to Louis de Wecker (Paris) and Otto Becker (Heidelberg).
    • Friendships: Ferdinand Blumentritt (Austria), Maximo Viola, Juan Luna, Felix Hidalgo.
  • Propaganda Movement: Active writer and advocate for reforms, not separation.

Noli Me Tángere: The Awakening (1887)

  • Completed and published "Noli Me Tángere" in Berlin, 1887, with financial help from Maximo Viola.
  • A scathing indictment of the abuses of Spanish friars and the colonial government.
  • Characters like Crisostomo Ibarra, Maria Clara, Padre Damaso, Sisa, Basilio, and Crispin became symbols of Filipino society.
  • Caused an uproar in the Philippines; deemed heretical and subversive by friars.

A Brief Return, A Renewed Threat (1887-1888)

  • Returned to Calamba in August 1887, practicing medicine and defending his novel.
  • Ordered to leave by Governor-General Emilio Terrero due to threats from friars.
  • Departed for Hong Kong in February 1888.
  • Visited Japan and the United States, observing societal conditions.

Second Sojourn in Europe & El Filibusterismo (1888-1891)

  • London (1888-1889): Annotated Antonio de Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas."
  • Paris (1889-1890): Organized the "Kidlat Club" and "Indios Bravos"; contributed to "La Solidaridad."
  • Brussels & Ghent (1890-1891): Struggled financially while finishing "El Filibusterismo."
  • "El Filibusterismo": Published in Ghent, 1891, with help from Valentin Ventura. A more revolutionary, bitter novel exploring the consequences of societal decay.

Hong Kong & Founding La Liga Filipina (1891-1892)

  • Moved to Hong Kong in 1891, practicing as an ophthalmologist.
  • Planned the "Borneo Colonization Project" (New Calamba), but was denied by the Governor-General.
  • Drafted the constitution of La Liga Filipina, a civic organization promoting unity, mutual protection, education, and reforms.
  • Despite warnings, decided to return to the Philippines, believing his presence was crucial.

Arrest, Exile in Dapitan (1892-1896)

  • Returned to Manila in June 1892, established La Liga Filipina.
  • Arrested just days later on charges of sedition and for anti-friar pamphlets found in his sister's luggage.
  • Exiled to Dapitan, Mindanao, for four years.
  • Life in Dapitan:
    • Practiced medicine, built a school, developed irrigation systems.
    • Engaged in farming, fishing, and collecting scientific specimens.
    • Met Josephine Bracken, who became his common-law wife.
    • Corresponded with European scientists.

The Katipunan & Rizal's Imprisonment (1896)

  • The Katipunan, a secret revolutionary society founded by Andres Bonifacio, sought Rizal's blessing for an uprising. Rizal advised caution and proper preparation.
  • In July 1896, Rizal volunteered to serve as a doctor in Cuba during its revolution.
  • While en route to Cuba, the Philippine Revolution erupted (Cry of Pugad Lawin, August 1896).
  • Rizal was arrested aboard his ship in Barcelona, Spain, and sent back to Manila under heavy guard.

The Mock Trial: A Foregone Conclusion (1896)

  • Imprisoned at Fort Santiago.
  • Accused of rebellion, sedition, and forming illegal associations.
  • A military court found him guilty, despite insufficient evidence linking him directly to the armed revolution.
  • Sentenced to death by firing squad.

The Final Hours & Mi Último Adiós (December 29-30, 1896)

  • Spent his final hours writing letters, receiving family visits.
  • Wrote "Mi Último Adiós" (My Last Farewell), a powerful, unsigned poem smuggled out by his sister.
  • The poem expressed his unwavering love for his country and his willingness to die for its freedom.
  • Married Josephine Bracken hours before his execution (disputed by some historians, but accepted by many).

Execution at Bagumbayan (December 30, 1896)

  • Led from Fort Santiago to Bagumbayan (now Rizal Park).
  • Refused to be blindfolded; requested to face his executioners, but was shot in the back.
  • His last words: "Consummatum est!" (It is finished!).
  • His death ignited the revolution further, uniting Filipinos against Spanish rule.

The Enduring Legacy

  • Inspired not just the Philippine Revolution but also future generations of Filipino leaders and activists.
  • His ideals of peaceful reform, intellectual pursuit, and national identity remain cornerstones of Filipino patriotism.
  • Recognized as the National Hero of the Philippines.
  • Numerous monuments, schools, and institutions bear his name worldwide.
